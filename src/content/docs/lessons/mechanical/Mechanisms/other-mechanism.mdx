The drivetrain is one of the most important components of the robot, as it dictates the speed, maneuverability, and overall reliability of the machine. However, it is only one part of a much larger system. Every subsystem is designed with a specific purpose, and together they enable the robot to complete complex game tasks efficiently. The following sections introduce the robot's major subsystems—including the bellypan, intake, indexer, elevator, spindexer, and other key mechanisms—and explain how each contributes to the robot's overall performance.

---

## Bellypan

The bellypan serves as the structural foundation of the robot and is mounted directly to the drivetrain, providing a rigid frame that supports the robot's mechanical and electrical systems. It acts as the primary mounting platform for critical electrical components, including the RoboRIO, Power Distribution Hub (PDH), motor controllers, radio, battery, and other electronics.

Beyond simply holding components, the bellypan plays a significant role in the robot's structural integrity. By tying together the drivetrain rails, it increases chassis rigidity, allowing the robot to better withstand the impacts and stresses of competition. Proper placement of heavy components, such as the battery, helps lower the robot's center of gravity, improving stability and reducing the likelihood of tipping during aggressive acceleration, defense, or climbing.

A well-designed bellypan also emphasizes organization. Clean wire routing, clearly labeled components, and accessible mounting locations make troubleshooting and repairs significantly faster during competitions, where every minute between matches is valuable. Bellypans are commonly manufactured from aluminum sheet because of its excellent balance of strength, weight, and machinability, although composite materials such as polycarbonate or carbon fiber may also be used depending on the team's design priorities.
